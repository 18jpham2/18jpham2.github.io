Martin Hellman

Read my new book! A New Map For Relationships

NYC, New York | Professor Emeritus of Electrical Engineering

Inventor of Public Key Encryption

Current Retired Book Author
Previous Professor of Electrical Engineering at Stanford University
Education Bronx High School of Science, B.E.E at New York University, M.S.E.E and Ph.D at Standford University
Public Key Encryption

Public Key Encryption works by having two computers generate two keys, a public and a private one. They can exchange secret messages by encrypting the information using a public key and the other decrypts it by combining their private key with the public one.
New Directions in Cryptography

A paper written by Diffie and Hellman on November 1976 which was submitted to the IEEE. This is a groundbreaking paper that introduced a new method of distributing cryptographic key. This includes the Diffie-Hellman Key exchange revolutionized the way people communicate and transfer data.

Diffie-Hellman Key Exchange

Diffie-Hellman key exchange is important because it allows people to share secret information with each other without enabling observers to discover the contents or necessary keys to gain access to them.
Timeline:
  • Born in New York, 1945
  • Graduated from Bronx High School of Science in 1962
  • Received a Bachelor in electrical engineering from New York University in 1966
  • Received a Master in 1967 and a Ph. D. in 1969 from Stanford
  • Left IBM to teach in Electrical Engineering at MIT in 1969
  • Published New Directions in Cryptography in 1976
  • Played a leading role in the late 1970s' and early 1980s' first ''crypto war''
  • Worked with Soviet scientists in the 1980s to reduce nuclear weapons
  • Won the IEEE Centennial Medal in 1984
  • Won the Electronic Frontier Foundation Pioneer Award in 1994
  • Became professor emeritus in electrical engineering at Stanford University in 1996
  • Was elected to the National Academy of Engineering in 2002
  • Won the ACM Turing Award (Nobel Prize of Computer Science) in 2015
Contributions to Computer Science:
  • Hellman invented public key cryptography with his colleagues, Diffie and Merkle. The Diffie-Hellman key exchange was one of the first public-key protocols and was succeeded by RSA. This is important because it enables secure Internet transactions which involve the transferring of trillions of dollars everyday. Their insights enable companies and individuals to transfer and communicate data safely in this digital age.
  • Another contribution by Hellman is that he strongly advocated for cryptographic researchers' right to publish their work and the public's right to strong encryption that can resist agencies such as the National Security Agency (NSA). He was against the Data Encryption Standard which the NSA has endorsed to use brute force against it. His concern for the right to privacy of the people is noble and should be held as an example in which freedom triumphs surveillance.
